# LawSignal — Identity

## Purpose, method, end state

**Purpose.** Help people make clear, defensible decisions about
where to apply and attend law school under real-world constraints
— limited time, imperfect information, decisions that feel
permanent.

**Method.** Structure the information, expose the tradeoffs, and
guide the choice through defaults that respect the user's agency.

**End state.** Users act with clarity, own their decisions, and
walk away with something they can defend to themselves a year
later.

## Why this exists

The law school admissions process is broken in predictable ways.
Applicants are making six-figure, career-shaping decisions with
scattered information — rankings that flatten schools into a single
number, marketing copy that reads identically across 200 programs,
employment data buried in ABA disclosures that most applicants
never open, and scholarship offers that expire before the picture
is clear.

The people who navigate this well got there through lawyer parents,
expensive admissions consultants, or a forum habit they don't have
time for. The structural problem is the same one David Lat names in
legal recruiting: information asymmetry is lose-lose — schools get
applicants who didn't understand the fit, applicants get outcomes
they didn't anticipate, and the profession loses before anyone's
first class. Information earlier, given equally, fixes more than it
breaks.

LawSignal redistributes that information. Free, independent,
opinionated about methodology, no door fee.

## Who the user is

Three users, one structural problem.

**The fog user** (pre-law student building an apply list):
- Cannot name 20 law schools outside the T14
- Has no lawyer in the family or the close network
- Is anxious about the cycle and feels the clock
- About to spend $3,000+ on applications for reasons they cannot
  fully defend
- Has nobody to call who has been through this recently

**The list user** (admitted student choosing between offers):
- Has 2–5 acceptances with different scholarship packages
- Cannot easily compare total cost of attendance across schools
- Is making a 3-year, six-figure bet on their career trajectory
- Knows the rankings but not what the numbers behind them mean for
  their specific goals
- Has more vocabulary than the fog user but the same information
  gap on schools outside their shortlist

**The learner** (anyone exploring the law school landscape):
- Is not making a decision today
- Wants to understand what differentiates schools — not in
  marketing terms, in the terms an informed applicant would use
- Will leave with a sharper mental model rather than a list
- Might be a college sophomore, a career changer weighing law
  school, a pre-law advisor, or a 0L lurking forums
- Is the use case the editorial profiles were always for

Admissions consultants and pre-law advisors are welcome but not
designed for. When the fog user and the list user pull in different
directions, both get a path; when ANY of them pulls against a
consultant or a school's admissions office, the applicant wins.

## Two emotional states on arrival

The fog user arrives overwhelmed: "I'm studying for the LSAT and I
don't know what schools to target, how many to apply to, or what
actually matters beyond the ranking number."

The list user arrives with a deadline: "I have offers from School A,
School B, and School C. Tell me how to think about this before April
15 without just chasing the highest-ranked name."

The site has to recognize which one walked in and meet them there.
Not with a marketing pitch. With a clear sequence: this is what you
came for, this is the next step, this is what you'll walk away with.

## What success looks like for one session

A session succeeds if the user leaves with BOTH:

1. **Confidence in a decision they were already half-making.** A
   defensible framework. Numbers they generated themselves. A set of
   priorities they articulated before they looked at the schools, so
   the ranking didn't bias the priorities. Something they could
   explain to a pre-law advisor without flinching.

2. **A concrete output sized to their decision.** For the fog user,
   a defensible apply list of roughly 8–20 schools tiered into
   "target / reach / safety" with a clear reason for each tier. For
   the list user, a focused comparison of 2–5 schools they're
   actively choosing between, weighted by what they said matters.
   In both cases, at least one school in the output is one they
   hadn't considered when they arrived.

They do NOT leave with: a single "best" school, a prediction of
admission chances, admissions consulting, a promise.

## What the tool refuses to be

- Not a ranking authority. US News already exists. We weight YOUR
  priorities.
- Not a marketing channel. Schools can't pay to move up, can't pay
  to be included, can't pay to soften language. There is nothing to
  buy.
- Not a data dashboard. Editorial, not analytical. The fonts are
  deliberate. The tone is deliberate. This sits with Law School
  Transparency, LSAC, ABA 509 reports, Spivey Consulting's public
  writing — not with a SaaS product.
- Not admissions consulting or legal advice. We give you the map
  and the technique. You walk it.
- Not a closed box. The methodology is visible. The scores are
  editable. Disagreeing with the tool is using the tool correctly.

## Professional identity

LawSignal sits in the room with ABA 509 disclosures, US News
rankings, Law School Transparency reports, LSAC data, and Spivey
Consulting's public analyses. That's the neighborhood. Editorial
legal-education reference, not product marketing. The typography is
meant to earn the credibility the decision requires.

If an applicant shows this to a pre-law advisor and the advisor
thinks "oh, this is serious," the visual language did its job.

## The technique

The author's mom made him do weighted decision matrices for big life
choices. The technique is: name your priorities BEFORE you look at
the options, weight them honestly, then score the options against
those weights, and override scores you disagree with after looking at
them carefully. Picking a law school is a big life choice. This tool
is that technique, scaled to 200+ ABA-accredited schools, given away
free, because the people who most need it are the ones who were never
taught to make one.

## The method: choice architecture, not advice

LawSignal does not tell users which school to pick. It builds the
choice architecture that helps them make a decision they can defend
to themselves. The framing is borrowed from Thaler and Sunstein's
*Nudge* — libertarian paternalism, the idea that you can structure
a choice environment to make the better decision easier without
removing any options or imposing your judgment on the chooser.

In practice that means:

- **Defaults that work for the median user.** The mode preset on
  the questionnaire fills in priorities so a user who clicks through
  without thinking still gets a defensible starting point. The
  default is the nudge.
- **Inviting deliberation without requiring it.** The Priorities
  screen asks the user to name what matters BEFORE they look at
  schools. They can skip the slider exercise — clicking through
  without touching anything is a legitimate path — but the screen
  exists to make the deliberation easy and visible.
- **Surfacing dissent.** The sensitivity panel ("if you weighted
  employment outcomes 20% higher, two schools move up") is a
  structured invitation to second-guess the result. The tool earns
  trust by arguing against itself.
- **No path to a single answer.** The output is always a list, a
  matrix, or a defensible framework — never a "best school." The
  user makes the call; the tool gives them the architecture.

The mom-rule technique (priorities BEFORE options, weight them
honestly, override what you disagree with) is the core nudge: the
sequence of the questionnaire is not arbitrary, it's the choice
architecture that prevents the most common failure mode of
decision-making under uncertainty — reverse-engineering your
priorities to match a preordained answer.

This matters because people making big decisions under uncertainty
rarely fail from lack of intelligence. They fail because the
systems around them are designed for someone else, or for no one
in particular. LawSignal is built for users operating in the real
world — limited time, imperfect information, decisions that feel
permanent — and the choice architecture is the load-bearing thing.
The tool's job is not to be smart. It is to be designed.

## The four anchors

Every UX decision should serve at least one of these. If it serves
none, cut it.

1. **Credibility** — earn the trust the decision requires
2. **Clarity** — one thing to do at a time, in order
3. **Calm** — a person making a hard decision should feel less
   anxious after using this, not more
4. **Discovery** — surface schools and ideas the user didn't know to
   ask for, without ever feeling like a marketing pitch. For users
   who aren't actively choosing — college students building
   intuition, career changers exploring the landscape, pre-law
   advisors looking for reference material, anyone trying to
   understand what makes one school different from another — this is
   the whole point, not a side benefit.

## Principles

The four anchors are tests for individual UI choices. These
principles are the substrate the anchors come from — the operating
beliefs that shape what the tool is willing to do and what it
refuses to do. When in doubt about a choice, check it against
both: does it serve at least one anchor, and does it honor every
principle?

- **Clarity over volume.** More information is not better. Better
  structure is. The tool gives the user fewer choices early and
  more depth on demand, never the reverse. The sensitivity panel,
  the methodology page, the full slider set, the editorial profiles
  — all powerful, all one click away, none in the user's face by
  default.

- **Decisions are made.** The user does not stumble into the
  right school. They define the objective, constrain the problem,
  weight what matters, and choose. The questionnaire is the
  technique, not a search box.

- **Default environments shape outcomes.** People follow the path
  of least resistance. The defaults LawSignal ships with — mode
  presets, equal-weight starts, the ordering of the questionnaire,
  the visible-vs-advanced split — are chosen, not inherited. Every
  default is a nudge.

- **Agency is non-negotiable.** Defaults guide; they do not
  decide. Every score is editable, every ranking is overridable,
  every assumption is visible, every methodology choice is
  defended in plain English. The user owns the outcome.

- **Everything should survive scrutiny.** If a recommendation
  cannot be explained, defended, and stress-tested, it does not
  belong in the tool. Methodology is visible, sources are named,
  scores are editable, the tool argues with itself via the
  sensitivity panel. The right test for any feature is whether a
  pre-law advisor could open it without rolling their eyes.

- **Real world, not theory.** People fail not because they lack
  intelligence but because the systems around them are designed
  for someone else, or for no one in particular. LawSignal is
  built for users operating under pressure, with limited time and
  imperfect information, who need to act decisively without being
  perfect. The job is not to be smart. It is to be designed.
