# Decision Analysis: Berkeley vs. Georgetown vs. Vanderbilt

A retrospective analysis of the author's law school decision using
the LawSignal framework. This serves as both a test case for the
scoring methodology and a reference for what the tool should be
able to surface.

---

## The Decision

**Chosen:** Berkeley Law (ranked #9 at decision time)
**Runner-up:** Vanderbilt Law (#17)
**Third option:** Georgetown Law (#14, pending interview)
**In reserve:** William & Mary, FSU

**Decision maker profile:**
- 25-year-old Army Infantry Officer, West Point graduate
- FLEP (Funded Legal Education Program) — Army pays tuition up to
  $28,000/year, officer continues receiving full pay + BAH
- Single, no dependents
- Owned a house in Nashville near Vanderbilt
- No lawyer in the family network
- Interested in IP law, nuclear energy law (long-term)
- Planning to serve 6+ years JAG after law school
- Eligible to leave Army at 34 (2033)

---

## Dimension-by-Dimension Scoring

### 1. Selectivity

| School | Median LSAT | Median GPA | Accept Rate | 1L Class |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Berkeley | ~170 | ~3.8 | ~20% | 278 |
| Georgetown | ~170 | ~3.8 | ~22% | 593 |
| Vanderbilt | ~168 | ~3.8 | ~25% | 155 |

**Winner: Berkeley ≈ Georgetown > Vanderbilt**

Berkeley and Georgetown are similarly selective; Vanderbilt is
slightly less so. But selectivity wasn't a differentiator in this
decision — all three are excellent schools that would signal peer
caliber to employers.

### 2. Employment Outcomes

All three schools place well. For a FLEP officer, standard
employment outcomes are less directly relevant because the
post-grad path is predetermined (JAG). Employment matters as:
- Signal of school quality
- Optionality for post-service career (BigLaw, clerkship, GS)
- Network breadth

**Winner: Berkeley > Georgetown > Vanderbilt** (for national
optionality; Vanderbilt would win for South/Midwest placement)

### 3. Cost & Value

| School | BAH 1L/mo | Annual 1L Income | State |
|---|---|---|---|
| Berkeley | $3,501 | $94,135 | CA |
| Georgetown | $2,679 | $84,283 | DC |
| Vanderbilt | $2,076 | $77,047 | TN |

Army pays tuition up to $28K — **Berkeley's willingness to cap at
$28K was the binary constraint.** If Berkeley said no, the
decision was made for him.

Higher BAH at Berkeley partially offsets higher COL. Nashville
housing was already owned (mortgage ~$1,350). Berkeley = $2,000+
for a 500sf apartment vs. Vanderbilt = 1,440sf owned house.

**Winner on paper: Vanderbilt** (lowest COL, owned housing, most
financial cushion)
**Winner on constraint satisfaction: Berkeley** (IF they agree
to tuition cap — which they did)

### 4. Geographic Strength

| School | Primary placement market | Portability |
|---|---|---|
| Berkeley | Bay Area / West Coast | Strong national, strongest West |
| Georgetown | DC / Mid-Atlantic | Strong national, strongest DC |
| Vanderbilt | Nashville / South / Midwest | Good national, strongest South |

The author intended to "practice in the [DC] area at some point
later on" — Georgetown would have been the slam dunk for that.
But Berkeley's West Coast network was something he couldn't get
any other way.

**Winner: depends on target geography.**
- For DC: Georgetown
- For West Coast: Berkeley
- For South/Midwest: Vanderbilt
- For national optionality: Berkeley > Georgetown > Vanderbilt

### 5. Academic Quality

| School | S:F Ratio | Faculty | 1L Sections | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Berkeley | 5.5:1 | 284 | Mods (30) + super mods (90) | "Incredibly gifted, less accessible" |
| Vanderbilt | 4.7:1 | 143 | Traditional small | "Accessible, invested in success" |
| Georgetown | 3.2:1 | 812 | Large | Largest law school in US |

Georgetown's 3.2:1 ratio is deceptive — the sheer size (2,603
students) means the experience is very different from Vanderbilt's
673. Berkeley's mod system provides small-group experience within
a medium school.

**Winner: Vanderbilt** (best ratio, most accessible faculty,
most mentorship-oriented). Berkeley close second on intellectual
caliber. Georgetown third due to scale.

### 6. Prestige & Reputation

| School | Rank | Tier | "Name" value (author) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Berkeley | 9 | T10 | "Berkeley Law is an immediate attention grabber" |
| Georgetown | 14 | T14 | "Very strong name in DC" |
| Vanderbilt | 17 | T20 | "Good name" |

The author valued prestige not as a ranking but as a **signal
amplifier**: "Infantry Officer from West Point with Berkeley Law"
was the combination that grabbed attention. This is a key insight
— prestige interacts with personal background.

**Winner: Berkeley > Georgetown > Vanderbilt**

### 7. Culture & Community

| School | Grading | Culture | Vet community |
|---|---|---|---|
| Berkeley | Pass/fail | Collaborative, diverse, "weirder" | Smaller but present |
| Vanderbilt | Generous curve | Collaborative, collegial, tight | Strong FLEP network |
| Georgetown | Traditional curve | Competitive (reputation) | Largest veteran contingent |

Berkeley's pass/fail system was a significant factor — "would put
me in a great spot to take a knee and explore the area." The author
valued exploration over optimization.

Vanderbilt's FLEP network was unique and valuable. Georgetown's
veteran community was the largest. But the author wasn't optimizing
for military network — he was optimizing for diversity of thought.

**Winner: Berkeley** (pass/fail + diversity of thought + "most
impressive student population"). Vanderbilt close second on
community warmth.

### 8. Quality of Life

| School | City | Housing | Weather | Social |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Berkeley | SF Bay Area | 500sf apartment, $2K+ | Best | "Not as fun" (but SF) |
| Vanderbilt | Nashville | 1440sf owned house | Good | "Too fun" |
| Georgetown | DC | TBD (family housing option) | OK | "Rat race" |

Nashville was the most comfortable and fun — which the author
identified as a *risk*: "I may not grow a lot here. Maybe I will
learn a lot, but I could easily get trapped in the Nashville
lifestyle and not grow up."

SF was "the only time in my life I would live in a big city like
San Francisco." This is a key quality-of-life consideration that
transcends the numbers.

**Winner on comfort: Vanderbilt >> Georgetown > Berkeley**
**Winner on "3-year life experience": Berkeley > Georgetown > Vanderbilt**

### 9. Growth & Fit

This is where the decision was actually made.

| School | Comfort zone disruption | Growth potential | Author's read |
|---|---|---|---|
| Berkeley | Maximum — new city, no network, across the country | Highest | "Stress my systems and create a new network" |
| Georgetown | Medium — near family, new city | Medium | "Safe and fun option" |
| Vanderbilt | Minimum — owned house, established routines | Lower | "Comfortable take stride... enjoy life" |

The author's central question: "what person I want it to make me."

Berkeley's admissions offer was itself a signal: "By granting me
admittance, Berkeley has given me a large vote of confidence. That
seriously draws me to the school because they think I would be a
good fit." Vanderbilt's waitlist was the opposite signal.

**Winner: Berkeley** — and it's the decisive dimension.

## The Decision Matrix (as LawSignal would render it)

If the author had LawSignal and weighted his priorities as revealed
by the document:

| Dimension | Weight | Berkeley | Vanderbilt | Georgetown |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Selectivity | 5% | 88 | 78 | 85 |
| Employment | 10% | 90 | 82 | 87 |
| Cost & Value | 10% | 65 | 90 | 75 |
| Geographic | 10% | 85 | 70 | 80 |
| Academic | 10% | 82 | 88 | 72 |
| Prestige | 15% | 92 | 75 | 82 |
| Culture | 15% | 90 | 85 | 70 |
| Quality of Life | 10% | 72 | 88 | 78 |
| **Growth & Fit** | **15%** | **95** | **60** | **72** |
| **Weighted Score** | 100% | **85.7** | **78.7** | **77.6** |

Berkeley wins by ~7 points. The tiebreaker is Growth & Fit (15%
weight) where Berkeley scores 95 vs. Vanderbilt's 60.

**Sensitivity check:** If Growth & Fit is weighted at 0% (the
user doesn't care about personal growth — they just want the
most comfortable path):

| Without Growth | Berkeley | Vanderbilt | Georgetown |
|---|---|---|---|
| Reweighted score | ~83 | ~83 | ~79 |

Berkeley and Vanderbilt tie. The growth dimension IS the decision.

---

## What LawSignal should surface from this

1. **The sensitivity panel should catch this.** "If you weighted
   Growth & Fit 15% lower, Vanderbilt moves into a virtual tie
   with Berkeley." This is exactly the kind of insight that helps
   a user understand their own decision.

2. **The editorial brief for each school should name the tradeoff.**
   Berkeley's brief: "Forces growth — new city, new network, out
   of comfort zone. Pass/fail grading gives room to explore."
   Vanderbilt's brief: "Warm community, strong FLEP network,
   comfortable transition. The question is whether comfort is what
   you need right now."

3. **The cost filter should support constraints.** "Only show
   schools that meet [specific tuition cap]" — this is a
   dealbreaker, not a gradient.

4. **The tool should not have made this decision for the author.**
   It should have surfaced the data, made the tradeoffs visible,
   and let the author see that Growth & Fit was his tiebreaker
   BEFORE he looked at the schools. That's the mom-rule.
